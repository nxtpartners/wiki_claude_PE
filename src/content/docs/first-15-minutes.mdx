The fastest way to understand Claude is to use it on something real. In the next fifteen minutes you'll go from a blank screen to a one-page summary of an actual document from your desk, and you'll see exactly why this becomes a daily habit.

<Callout type="tip" title="Pick a low-stakes document">
For this first run, choose something useful but not highly sensitive: a public teaser, an industry report, or an old deck. You'll learn the workflow without worrying about confidentiality while you find your feet. The data rules come next, in Foundations.
</Callout>

## Your first session

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open Claude and start a new chat">
    Go to <strong>claude.ai</strong> and sign in with your KPMG account. You'll land on a chat screen with a single message box. That box is the whole interface: you simply type, or attach a file, and press send.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Attach a document">
    Click the attachment (paperclip) icon and upload a PDF, Word doc, or Excel file. Claude reads the entire file: a 60-page CIM is no problem. Wait for the file to finish attaching before you send.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Ask with clear instructions">
    Don't just say "summarise this." Tell Claude who it's helping, what you want, and how to format it. Paste the prompt below; it works for almost any deal document.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read, then push back">
    Skim the output. Then ask follow-ups in the same chat: "Expand the risks section," "What's missing?", "Put the financials in a table." Claude remembers everything in the conversation, so each message builds on the last.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Verify before you use anything">
    Check any figure or claim against the original document before it goes near a deliverable. Claude is fast and fluent, but it can be confidently wrong, so the source is always the final word.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## The prompt to start with

<PromptCard title="One-page summary of any deal document">
You are assisting a Private Equity associate at KPMG. I've attached a document for a potential opportunity.

Give me a one-page summary using these headings:
1. Business overview: what they do and how they make money
2. Financial highlights: revenue, EBITDA, growth, margins
3. Why it could be attractive: the investment angle
4. Key risks and open questions

Use bullet points. Keep it concise. Flag anything the document is vague about or that looks unusual.
</PromptCard>

Notice what made that prompt work: it set a **role** ("assisting a PE associate"), gave a **clear task**, specified the **format** (headings, bullets), and asked Claude to **flag gaps**. That four-part shape (role, task, format, scrutiny) is the backbone of almost every good prompt, and the [Anatomy of a Good Prompt](/foundations/anatomy-of-a-prompt) page breaks it down properly.

## Try one more turn

The real power shows up in the second message. With the summary on screen, try one of these:

- *"Turn the financial highlights into a table with year-on-year growth."*
- *"What are the three questions I should ask management first?"*
- *"Rewrite the overview for a partner who has thirty seconds."*

Each reply builds on the document and everything said so far, with no need to re-explain.

<Callout type="confidential" title="Before you use a live deal document">

## What you just learned

In one short session you've seen the entire core loop: **give context → ask precisely → refine → verify**. Everything else in this guide makes that loop faster and more powerful: better prompts, reusable Projects, and playbooks tuned to specific PE tasks.
