When a teaser lands at 6pm and a partner wants a read by morning, you do not want to be composing a prompt from scratch. This is the page to bookmark for exactly those moments. Below is a working collection of prompts for the tasks you do most often, each built on the four-part shape (**Role, Task, Format, Scrutiny**) from [Anatomy of a Good Prompt](/foundations/anatomy-of-a-prompt). They are starting points, not scripts. Paste one in, adjust the specifics to your situation, attach the relevant document, and refine from there.

<Callout type="confidential" title="Check before you upload">
Every prompt here assumes you are in the KPMG Enterprise or Team workspace and that the document you are attaching is permitted for processing. Make that call first using the [Confidentiality Decision Tree](/best-practices/confidentiality). And whatever the prompt produces, verify it against the source before it reaches a deliverable, as [Verify Everything](/best-practices/verify-everything) explains.
</Callout>

## Reading and summarising documents

The bread and butter: turning a long document into something you can act on quickly, without losing the things that matter.

<PromptCard title="One-page CIM or teaser summary">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate. I have attached an information pack for Project Atlas, a mid-market B2B field-service software business.

Summarise it for an initial screen using these headings:
1. Business overview: what they do and how they make money
2. Financial highlights: revenue, EBITDA, growth, margins
3. Investment thesis: why this could be attractive
4. Key risks and open questions

Use bullets under each heading and keep it to one page. Flag anything the document is vague about, and call out anything that looks unusual or that I should press management on.
</PromptCard>

<PromptCard title="Extract the key financial figures">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate. From the attached document, pull out the headline financials into a single table: revenue, gross margin, EBITDA, EBITDA margin, year-on-year growth, and net debt, for each year shown.

For every figure, note the page or section it came from. If a figure is not stated, write "not stated" rather than estimating. After the table, flag any number that looks inconsistent with the others or that depends on an assumption I should check.
</PromptCard>

## Risk and due diligence

Surfacing what could go wrong, and turning a data room into a focused list of things to chase.

<PromptCard title="Extract and rank the risks">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate reviewing Project Atlas. From the attached document, identify the material risks to this investment.

Group them under: commercial, financial, operational, and legal/regulatory. Under each, give the risk in one line, then a one-line "so what" explaining why it would matter to the investment case. Rank the whole list so the most deal-relevant risks come first. If the document hints at a risk without stating it plainly, flag that as something to confirm rather than as fact.
</PromptCard>

<PromptCard title="Build a due-diligence question list">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate. Based on the attached document for Project Atlas, draft the due-diligence questions I should put to the seller and to management.

Organise them by workstream (commercial, financial, technology, people, legal). For each question, add a short note on why it matters and what a concerning answer would look like. Prioritise the questions that could change the thesis. Mark any question that arises because the document is silent or unclear on a point.
</PromptCard>

## Market and competitive work

Framing the market and the competitive picture, kept honest about what is established fact versus general knowledge.

<PromptCard title="Market sizing and framing">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate. I am assessing the market for Project Atlas, a B2B field-service software business serving mid-market customers.

Lay out how to think about the market size and growth: the relevant segments, the main demand drivers, and the structural trends that would support or undermine a thesis here. Be explicit about what is general industry knowledge versus what would need primary data to confirm. Do not invent specific market-size figures. End with the three data points I would most want before relying on any sizing.
</PromptCard>

<PromptCard title="Competitive comparison table">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate. Compare Project Atlas against two or three typical competitors in mid-market field-service software.

Present it as a table with these rows: target customer, product breadth, pricing model, apparent strengths, apparent weaknesses, and the main competitive risk to Atlas. Where you are reasoning from general sector knowledge rather than the attached document, say so clearly. Write "not stated" for anything the materials do not cover.
</PromptCard>

## Management and process

Sharpening the questions you put to people, and preparing for the conversations that decide a deal.

<PromptCard title="Management meeting questions">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate preparing for a management meeting on Project Atlas. Based on the attached materials, draft the questions I should ask.

Group them by theme (growth and pipeline, unit economics, customer retention, team and key-person risk, the plan for the next three years). For each, add the one-line reason it matters and what answer would reassure me versus concern me. Put the must-ask questions first. Flag any question prompted by a gap or inconsistency in the materials.
</PromptCard>

## Writing and drafting

Turning analysis into the documents that carry it forward, in the right register.

<PromptCard title="Investment memo section">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate drafting an investment committee paper on Project Atlas. Using the attached analysis, write the investment thesis section.

Constraints: measured and specific tone suitable for an investment committee, no marketing language or superlatives, around 300 words, structured as thesis, value-creation angle, and the main risk to the thesis. Base every claim on the attached material; if something important is missing, list it separately below rather than inventing it.
</PromptCard>

<PromptCard title="Executive summary from a longer document">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate. Read the attached document and write a tight executive summary for a busy partner.

No more than 200 words, three short paragraphs (what it is, why it could be attractive, the main concern). Plain, confident, no filler. If the document leaves a key question open, name it in a single line at the end under "Open questions".
</PromptCard>

## Reshaping and refining

The smaller moves that save time once you already have a draft.

<PromptCard title="Rewrite in a different tone">
Take the text below and rewrite it for an investment committee audience: measured, precise, and free of marketing language, while keeping every substantive point intact. Do not add new claims or figures. If a sentence currently makes a claim that the underlying material would not support, flag it rather than rewriting around it.

[paste the text here]
</PromptCard>

<PromptCard title="Compare two versions or options">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate. Compare the two options below and help me decide.

Lay it out as a table: the dimensions that matter, how each option performs on each, and a final row with your one-line read on the trade-off. Be explicit about which dimensions are most decision-relevant. Where you are inferring rather than working from stated facts, label it.

Option A: [describe]
Option B: [describe]
</PromptCard>

## How to use these well

Three habits make the difference between a prompt that helps and one that hinders.

- **Adapt the specifics.** Swap Project Atlas for your situation, and make the role line match the real task.
- **Always keep the scrutiny instruction.** The "flag what is vague" and "not stated" lines are what keep the output honest. Do not delete them to make a prompt shorter.
- **Refine in place.** A first answer that is 80 percent right is normal. Correct the rest with a follow-up rather than starting over, as [Prompt Patterns](/best-practices/prompt-patterns) describes.

A single prompt gets you a fast, useful answer. A playbook strings several of them into a full workflow, from first screen to committee paper. When you are ready to go end to end, see [CIM Analysis](/playbooks/cim-analysis), [Due Diligence](/playbooks/due-diligence), [Market Research](/playbooks/market-research), [Financial Review](/playbooks/financial-review), and [Investment Memo](/playbooks/investment-memo). If a term in any prompt is unfamiliar, the [Glossary](/resources/glossary) has it in plain English.
