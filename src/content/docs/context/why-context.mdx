There is one idea that sits underneath everything else in this guide: Claude only knows what you tell it. It has no access to your live deal, your firm's house view, or the document open on your other screen unless you put that material in front of it. The quality of what you get back tracks, almost exactly, the quality of the context you give. Master that and the rest is technique.

## Claude starts with a blank page

When you open a new chat, Claude knows nothing about your work. It has broad general knowledge, but it cannot see your Project Atlas data room, it does not know which sector you are screening, and it has no idea what "the usual format" means to you. Every useful answer begins with you supplying the facts and the framing.

This is liberating once you internalize it. You are not hoping Claude guesses what you meant; you are deciding what it should know. A vague question forces Claude to invent the missing context, and invented context is where confident, fluent, wrong answers come from. A well briefed question gives Claude solid ground to stand on.

<Callout type="note" title="Context is the input you control">
You cannot control how Claude was trained, but you fully control what you put in the message box. That is the single biggest lever you have over output quality. Treat every prompt as a short brief to a sharp new analyst who has read nothing yet.
</Callout>

## The core loop

Good work with Claude is not a single perfect prompt. It is a short, repeatable loop that you run until the output is right. Four moves, in order, again and again.

<DiagramFigure image={coreLoop} alt="The core working loop: give context, ask precisely, refine, then verify, repeated 2 to 3 times until the output is right." caption="The core loop. Most people stop after the first answer; the value shows up on the second and third turn." maxWidth={640} />

- **Give context.** Attach the document, paste the figures, or state the situation. Tell Claude who it is helping and what it is working on.
- **Ask precisely.** State the task, the constraints, and the format you want. Precision in equals precision out.
- **Refine.** Read the first answer and push back in the same chat: expand a section, tighten the tone, challenge an assumption. The second turn is usually where the value appears.
- **Verify.** Check every figure, name, and claim against the source before any of it reaches a deliverable. Fluent prose is not the same as correct prose.

Most people stop after the first answer. The professionals run the loop two or three times, and the difference shows in the output.

## Context-free versus well briefed

The same underlying question produces wildly different answers depending on how much context comes with it. Compare these two openers on an identical document.

<PromptCard title="Context-free (weak)">
What do you think of this company?
</PromptCard>

Claude has no role, no task shape, no sense of what "good" looks like, and no idea what you will do with the answer. It will produce something generic and probably hedge in every direction.

<PromptCard title="Well briefed (strong)">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate screening Project Atlas, a mid-market field-service software target. I have attached the teaser.

Assess it on three dimensions, in this order:
1. Business quality (revenue model, growth, margins)
2. The investment angle (why this could be attractive to a buyer)
3. The three biggest risks or open questions

Use short bullet points. Cite the page or figure for any number you quote, and flag anything the teaser is vague about.
</PromptCard>

The second prompt sets a role, names the target, defines the task, fixes the format, and tells Claude to flag gaps. Same document, far more useful answer.

<ComparisonTable title="What context buys you" caption="The right column is what a few extra sentences of context produces.">

| With thin context | With rich context |
| --- | --- |
| Generic, hedged commentary | A focused view aimed at your actual decision |
| Claude guesses the format | Output arrives in the shape you can use |
| Invented detail fills the gaps | Claude flags gaps instead of inventing |
| You rewrite most of it | You refine the edges, not the whole thing |

</ComparisonTable>

## Context is cumulative within a chat

Once you have briefed Claude in a conversation, that context stays live. You do not re-attach the file or restate the situation on every turn. You simply keep going: "Now put the financials in a table," or "Rewrite the overview for a partner with thirty seconds." Each message builds on everything said so far, which is why refining is cheap and why a single well briefed chat compounds in value the longer you work in it.

The flip side matters too: because context accumulates, a chat that has drifted across three unrelated tasks becomes muddled ground for the fourth. Keep one chat to one job. The [What Not to Do](/best-practices/what-not-to-do) page returns to this.

## How to supply context

If context is everything, the next question is purely practical: how do you actually get your material in front of Claude? There are three routes, and most PE work uses the first two every day. The sections below walk through each, when to reach for it, and the one habit that keeps confidential material safe.

<ComparisonTable title="Attach, paste, or connect" caption="Most PE work lives in the first two rows. Connectors are an optional accelerator your IT team enables.">

| Method | When to use it | Strengths | Watch-outs |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Attach a file** | A teaser, CIM, model, or deck already saved on your machine | Reads whole documents (PDF, Word, Excel) in full; the everyday default | Confirm the file is permitted before uploading |
| **Paste text directly** | A few paragraphs, an email, a table, or notes with no file | Fast; no upload step; good for snippets | Long pastes get unwieldy; a file is cleaner past a page |
| **Connect a live source** | A large or frequently updated library (for example Google Drive) | Pulls live, large sources without re-uploading | Must be enabled by IT; always check the permissions on what you pull |

</ComparisonTable>

### Attach a file

Attaching is the workhorse. Claude reads the entire document, so a sixty-page CIM or a multi-tab Excel model is no problem. Click the plus button in the message box, choose **Upload from computer**, wait for the file to finish attaching, then ask your question. You can attach several files to one message, which is how you ask a single question across a whole set of documents.

### Paste text directly

Sometimes there is no file, just a chunk of text: a paragraph from an email, a short table, a few lines of management commentary. Paste it straight into the message box and tell Claude what to do with it. This is quicker than saving a file first, and it is ideal when the context is small. Past roughly a page, an attached file reads more cleanly and is easier to reference later.

### Connect a live source

Connectors let Claude reach an external system, such as a shared drive, without you downloading and re-uploading files by hand. They shine when the source is large or changes often. Two things to know up front: a connector has to be **switched on by your IT or workspace administrator** before it appears, and when you pull from it you should **check the permissions** on what you are accessing, exactly as you would on the underlying system. Connectors are an accelerator, not a requirement; the [Connectors](/power-features/connectors) page covers them in full.

## Asking across several documents

The real power of attaching is the cross-document question. Give Claude two or three files at once and ask it to reconcile, compare, or find the gaps between them.

<PromptCard title="Reconcile a model against the narrative">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate on Project Atlas. I have attached the teaser and the financial model.

Compare the two and tell me:
1. Where the model's growth and margin assumptions line up with the teaser's narrative
2. Where they diverge, with the figure and the source for each side
3. The questions these differences raise for management

Use a short table for the comparison. If a number appears in one file but not the other, say so rather than estimating it.
</PromptCard>

That single prompt does work that would otherwise mean flipping between two documents line by line, and it tells Claude to flag mismatches instead of papering over them.

## Confirm before you upload

Every method above puts your material into Claude, so the discipline is the same regardless of route: be deliberate about what goes in.

## The takeaway

Claude is a fast, capable partner, but it is only ever as good as the context you give it and only as trustworthy as your verification. Hold those two ends, rich context going in and disciplined checking coming out, and the loop in between does the work. In practice you will attach files most of the time, lean on multi-file questions to do cross-document work, and reach for connectors only once IT has switched them on. With the context supplied, the next lever is how you phrase the request itself, covered in [Writing Effective Instructions](/context/writing-instructions).
