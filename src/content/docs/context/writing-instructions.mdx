Supplying context gets the right material in front of Claude. Instructing well decides what Claude does with it. The gap between a mediocre answer and a partner-ready one is rarely the document; it is almost always the precision of the request. This page is the craft of that request.

## Five parts of a good instruction

Strong instructions tend to carry the same five elements. You do not need a rigid template, but if an answer disappoints, one of these is usually missing.

- **Role.** Who is Claude helping, and from what seat? "You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate" sets the register and the depth.
- **Task.** The single thing you want done, stated plainly. One clear job beats three vague ones.
- **Constraints.** The boundaries: length, scope, what to include, what to leave out, which source to rely on.
- **Format.** The shape of the output: headings, a table, bullet points, a one-paragraph summary. Decide it; do not leave it to chance.
- **What to do when unsure.** The instruction most people forget. Tell Claude to ask, or to flag, rather than guess. This is what turns a confident-but-wrong machine into a careful one.

<Callout type="tip" title="The fifth element earns its keep">
"If the document does not state something, say so rather than estimating" is the single most valuable line you can add to a deal prompt. It converts silent guesses into visible gaps, which is exactly what you want to see before anything reaches the investment committee.
</Callout>

## Before and after

The same task, instructed two ways. Notice that the second version is barely longer; it is just specific where the first is loose.

<ComparisonTable title="Loose versus precise" caption="The precise version does not work harder. It just removes every guess.">

| Loose instruction | Precise instruction |
| --- | --- |
| "Summarize this." | "In one page, summarize the attached CIM under four headings: business, financials, thesis, risks." |
| "Is the growth good?" | "Assess the revenue growth against the stated market growth rate; cite both figures and the source page." |
| "Make it sound better." | "Rewrite this paragraph for a partner: concise, neutral in tone, no superlatives, US English." |
| (no fallback) | "If a figure is not in the document, write 'not stated' rather than estimating it." |

</ComparisonTable>

## Building an instruction, step by step

When a task matters, assemble the request deliberately rather than typing the first thing that comes to mind.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Name the seat">
    Open with the role so Claude pitches at the right level: "You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate screening Project Atlas, a mid-market field-service software target."
  </Step>
  <Step title="State the one task">
    Say the single thing you want, in plain words. If you have three jobs, consider three messages, or number them explicitly so none gets dropped.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Set the constraints">
    Add the boundaries: how long, what to cover, what to ignore, and which source to trust. "Use only the attached files" keeps Claude from drifting into general knowledge.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Fix the format">
    Choose the output shape: a table, four headings, five bullets, a single paragraph. A defined format is easier to read and far easier to verify.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add the fallback">
    Close with what to do when unsure: "Ask me if anything is ambiguous, and flag (do not fill) any gap in the source." Now Claude surfaces uncertainty instead of hiding it.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## A worked example

Here is the full shape assembled into one instruction you can adapt for almost any screening task.

<PromptCard title="A precisely instructed screen">
You are assisting a KPMG private equity associate screening Project Atlas, a mid-market field-service software target. I have attached the teaser.

Task: produce a one-page initial screen.

Constraints: rely only on the attached document. Keep it to a single page. Cite the page number for any figure you quote.

Format: use these four headings, each with short bullets:
1. Business overview
2. Financial highlights
3. Investment angle
4. Key risks and open questions

When unsure: if the teaser does not state something, write "not stated" rather than estimating, and list anything ambiguous at the end as questions for management.
</PromptCard>

Every part is doing a job: the role sets depth, the task is singular, the constraints fence the answer, the format makes it usable, and the fallback turns silent guesses into a clean list of questions.

## Custom styles and house tone

One instruction deserves its own treatment: how you want the answer to sound. Claude can write in almost any register, which is useful and also a trap. Left to its own defaults it tends toward the enthusiastic and the wordy, neither of which belongs in an investment committee paper. Tone is an instruction, not a hope. State it the same way you state the task and the format, and a few words is usually enough: "concise, neutral, no superlatives, US English" reshapes an answer completely.

<ComparisonTable title="Default voice versus specified voice" caption="The same content, reshaped by a single line of tone instruction.">

| Claude's default tends to be | A specified house voice asks for |
| --- | --- |
| Enthusiastic, with superlatives | Measured and neutral |
| Wordy, with throat-clearing intros | Concise, straight to the point |
| Generic business register | Partner-ready, suited to an IC audience |
| Mixed spelling conventions | Consistent, for example US English |

</ComparisonTable>

The simplest way to control tone is to name it inside the request, alongside the role, task, and format you already specify.

<PromptCard title="A house-voice instruction">
You are drafting for a KPMG private equity audience. Write in our house voice throughout:

- Concise and partner-ready: no filler, no throat-clearing introductions.
- Neutral and measured: state findings plainly, avoid superlatives and hype.
- Precise: prefer specific figures and clear caveats over vague positives.
- US English spelling and conventions.

Now draft a three-paragraph summary of Project Atlas suitable for an initial investment committee read.
</PromptCard>

Pasting a tone block into every chat works, but it is repetitive and easy to vary by accident. For anything you do regularly, capture the voice once so it applies automatically. There are two clean ways to do this:

- **A saved Style.** Claude lets you capture a writing style and apply it to a conversation, so you select your house voice rather than retyping it. This is ideal for a personal, consistent register across one-off chats.
- **Standing instructions in a Project.** A Project holds instructions that apply to every conversation inside it. Put your house-voice block in the Project instructions and every memo, email, and summary you draft in that Project inherits the same tone without a single extra line. This is the better route for sustained deal work, because the voice and the deal context live together. See [Instructions for Claude](/context/instructions-for-claude) for how to write that standing block, the [Projects](/projects/what-projects-are) section for the setup, and [Instruction Templates](/projects/templates) for reusable starting points.

<Callout type="note" title="Consistency is the real prize">
An IC paper assembled from several Claude sessions reads badly if each section has a different voice: one breezy, one stiff, one littered with superlatives. Capturing the house voice once, in a Style or a Project, means every section sounds like it came from the same hand. That coherence is what makes a document feel partner-ready.
</Callout>

Specifying a measured, caveat-friendly voice does more than make prose look professional. A voice that prefers "the document states" over "this is a fantastic business" naturally surfaces the evidence and the gaps, which makes the output easier to verify. Hype hides uncertainty; a neutral register exposes it. The tone you ask for and the rigor you want tend to pull in the same direction.

## The takeaway

Precise instructions are not longer or more complex; they simply leave nothing for Claude to invent. Name the seat, state one task, set the constraints, fix the format, and say what to do when unsure. When you find yourself typing the same instructions on every deal, capture them once as standing [Instructions for Claude](/context/instructions-for-claude) so they apply automatically, and lock a consistent voice at the same time using the house-tone approach in the section above.
