The single most reassuring thing about Claude Code is also the easiest to miss: it never changes anything on your computer without asking you first. Every action passes through a short review, and you are the one who approves it. This is called the approval loop, and once you trust it, the tool stops feeling risky and starts feeling like a careful assistant.

## How the loop works

The loop has the same three beats every time, no matter how small or large the task.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Claude proposes">
    You make a request, and Claude replies with a plan rather than an action. It tells you what it intends to do, step by step, in plain language. For anything that touches a file, it also shows you the exact change: the old text and the new text, side by side, so you can see precisely what would move.
  </Step>
  <Step title="You review">
    You read the plan and the proposed changes. This is the heart of the loop. You are not rubber-stamping; you are checking that Claude understood the request and that the change is one you actually want. Take the time to read it, especially any figures or wording that will end up in front of others.
  </Step>
  <Step title="You approve or send it back">
    If it looks right, you approve, and only then does Claude make the change. If it looks wrong, incomplete, or simply not what you meant, you say no and explain. Claude adjusts and proposes again. You can go around this loop as many times as you need.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Nothing happens until you say yes

It is worth stating plainly, because it is the promise the whole tool rests on: no file on your computer is created, edited, renamed, or deleted until you approve the specific change. Reading your files and thinking about them happens freely; changing them does not. If you close the window at the review step, nothing has been altered.

This is what makes it safe to experiment. You can ask for something, see the proposed change, decide you do not like it, and walk away, all without a single mark on your actual documents.

<Callout type="confidential" title="What stays local">
Your files stay on your machine. Claude Code does not upload your folder. When you make a request, only your prompt and the specific context it needs (the parts of the files relevant to the task) are sent to Claude so it can reason about them. The documents themselves remain where they are, on your computer, under your control.
</Callout>

## Review is your job, not a formality

The approval loop only protects you if you actually use it. Approving without reading is the same as letting the tool run unsupervised, which defeats the point. Claude is capable and fast, but it can misread a request or make a mistake, and the review step is where you catch it.

<Callout type="warning" title="Verify every change">
Read what changed before you approve, do not just glance at it. If a change involves a number, a date, or a name, check it against the source document rather than trusting that it was carried over correctly. And use Git (covered later in this track) so that even an approved change can always be undone. Reviewing carefully and keeping an undo path are what let you move quickly without moving carelessly.
</Callout>

## The takeaway

The approval loop is simple: Claude proposes, you review the exact changes, and you approve or send it back. Nothing on your computer changes without your yes. Treat the review step as real work rather than a formality, verify anything that matters against the source, and lean on Git so every change stays reversible. With that habit in place, you stay firmly in control of everything Claude Code does.
