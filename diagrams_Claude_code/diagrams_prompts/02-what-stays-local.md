# Diagram 2: What stays on your machine

Diagram generation prompt (for Gemini or ChatGPT). Output image: `cc-stays-local.png`

---

Design a genuinely well made trust and boundary diagram for a private equity teaching guide. It shows the reader one reassuring thing: your files stay on your machine, and you stay in control. This is a client-facing concept graphic for KPMG Private Equity professionals, sitting inside a light, premium, institutional web page. Aim for world-class information design, the kind of calm, authoritative exhibit a senior compliance team would be glad to put in front of a partner. Calm, senior, precise, and clearly made on purpose.

**The concept to visualize:**

A clear boundary around your own computer, with your files inside it, and only the minimum crossing out to Claude.

- Your files and folders live on your computer and stay there. Show a clear enclosure (for example a dashed boundary) that represents your machine, with your files sitting safely inside it.
- When you ask Claude Code something, only your prompt plus the specific context it needs crosses that boundary out to Claude. The reasoning happens, and proposed changes come back to you for approval.
- Nothing is published or shared until you deliberately choose to. The default state is contained and safe.

The core feeling to land: containment and control. The boundary holds; only a thin, deliberate stream leaves it; and nothing goes live without your say-so.

**Required text labels (use these exactly, correctly spelled):**
- The boundary label, verbatim: "Your machine".
- The inside-the-boundary label, verbatim: "Your files stay here".
- The crossing-out label, verbatim: "Only your prompt and the context it needs go to Claude".
- The return label, verbatim: "Claude proposes changes".
- The control label, verbatim: "You approve before anything changes".
- The final assurance, verbatim: "Nothing goes live until you choose".

**Your creative freedom:**

You own the design. Decide the structure, the composition, the shape of the boundary, the arrangement of your files inside it, and the visual metaphor for the thin, deliberate stream that crosses out to Claude. Use a dashed enclosure, a soft-tint safe zone, a bounded panel, or any device you judge clearest and most elegant, as long as three things read at a glance: your files stay inside your machine, only the prompt and needed context cross the boundary, and nothing changes or goes live without your approval. Do not feel bound to any particular arrangement of zones, arrows, gates, or callouts. Let the inside feel contained and calm, and let the single crossing out feel small, intentional, and controlled. Make it beautiful and make it clear.

**Canvas and brand:**
- Light or white canvas (`#FFFFFF` or a very light tone). High resolution. Generous margins and quiet negative space.
- Brand palette, blue-led: deep blue `#00338D`, cobalt `#1E49E2`, sky `#ACEAFF`, teal `#00A3A1`, confidential red `#B42318`, ink `#0B1B33`, muted ink `#5A6678`, hairline `#E3E8EF`, white `#FFFFFF`, soft tint `#F5F7FA`. Lead with blue and ink. Use teal for the safe, approved element (for example the "you approve" or "your files stay here" moment). Reserve red only if it genuinely clarifies that nothing leaves without approval; otherwise leave it out.
- Typography: Inter or a near-identical clean grotesque. Real, legible, correctly spelled US English. Keep labels short.
- Depth is welcome and tasteful: soft shadow, subtle layering, gentle in-palette gradients, well-made containers. Never gaudy. Reference bar: world-class information design in the spirit of FT and Bloomberg graphics, Pentagram, and Stripe Press.

**Overall feel:**

A calm, reassuring boundary diagram: contained, controlled, and dimensional, with real craft in every label and edge. The reader should instantly understand that their files stay on their machine, that only the prompt and needed context leave, and that nothing goes live until they choose.

**Avoid:** dark or navy backgrounds, neon, purple or violet, sci-fi or circuit-board motifs, hexagon tech patterns, emoji or clip-art stock icons, childish or generic "AI infographic" looks, busy or noisy backgrounds, watermarks, and any misspelled or lorem text.
