# Diagram 2: The two-layer project system

Diagram generation prompt (for Gemini or ChatGPT).

---

Design a genuinely well made diagram of a two-layer project system for a private equity teaching guide. It shows the reader one organizing idea: there are two kinds of "Projects" in Claude, and they relate in a specific way. This is a client-facing concept graphic for KPMG Private Equity professionals, sitting inside a light, premium, institutional web page. Aim for world-class information design, the kind of organizing-principle exhibit a premium annual report would carry. Calm, senior, precise, and clearly made on purpose.

**The concept to visualize:**

Two layers of "Projects," and the relationship between them.

- One layer is a permanent, shared method that lives on across deals. It is called "Reusable function projects." It contains three standing members: Screening, Market research, Deal sourcing. This layer persists: it is the same method every time, shared across the team.
- The other layer is the live deals. It is called "Deal projects." There is one project per live deal, each is siloed and walled off from the others for confidentiality, and each one closes when its deal ends. Examples: Project Atlas, Project Bravo, Project Cyan, plus a placeholder standing in for future deals reading "and each new deal."

The core relationship to land: the reusable method is permanent and shared, while the deal projects are many, temporary, and isolated from one another. Put plainly: the method stays, the deals change.

**Required text labels (use these exactly, correctly spelled):**
- The two layer names, verbatim: "Reusable function projects" and "Deal projects".
- The three reusable members, verbatim: "Screening", "Market research", "Deal sourcing".
- The deal examples, verbatim: "Project Atlas", "Project Bravo", "Project Cyan", and the placeholder "and each new deal".
- The relationship idea, expressed clearly somewhere in the composition, for example "The method stays." and "The deals change."

**Your creative freedom:**

You own the design. Decide the visual arrangement, the composition, the node and container shapes, and the visual metaphor for the two layers. Stack them, place them side by side, nest one within the other, or use any structure you judge clearest and most elegant, as long as three things read at a glance: which layer is the permanent shared method, which layer is the live deals, and the fact that the deals are one-per-deal and siloed from each other while the method persists. Do not feel bound to any particular arrangement of registers, eyebrows, axes, grids, or annotation placement. Make the meaning instantly legible, and let the reusable layer feel solid and grounded while the deal projects feel lighter, provisional, and walled off. Make it beautiful and make it clear.

**Canvas and brand:**
- Light or white canvas (`#FFFFFF` or a very light tone). High resolution. Generous margins and negative space.
- Brand palette, blue-led: deep blue `#00338D`, cobalt `#1E49E2`, sky `#ACEAFF`, teal `#00A3A1`, confidential red `#B42318`, ink `#0B1B33`, muted ink `#5A6678`, hairline `#E3E8EF`, white `#FFFFFF`, soft tint `#F5F7FA`. Lead with blue and ink. This is not a decision diagram, so use teal and red sparingly if at all.
- Typography: Inter or a near-identical clean grotesque. Real, legible, correctly spelled US English. Keep labels short.
- Depth is welcome and tasteful: soft shadow, subtle layering, gentle in-palette gradients, well-made containers. Never gaudy. Reference bar: world-class information design in the spirit of FT and Bloomberg graphics, Pentagram, and Stripe Press.

**Overall feel:**

An organizing principle rendered with the calm and craft of a world-class exhibit: senior, dimensional, and instantly legible. The permanent shared method endures; the siloed deal projects are many, temporary, and close with the deal. The method stays, the deals change.

**Avoid:** dark or navy backgrounds, neon, purple or violet, sci-fi or circuit-board motifs, hexagon tech patterns, emoji or clip-art stock icons, childish or generic "AI infographic" looks, busy or noisy backgrounds, watermarks, and any misspelled or lorem text.
